Substack vs Buttondown: Detailed Comparison for Newsletter Creators

Both Substack and Buttondown are popular email marketing platforms founded in 2017, but they serve different audiences with distinct approaches. Substack focuses on monetization and building an audience, while Buttondown caters to developers and technical writers who prefer markdown-based writing. Your choice depends on your technical comfort level, monetization goals, and subscriber base size.

Choose Substack if...

  • You want to monetize your newsletter with minimal upfront costs through revenue-share
  • You're starting out and need to reach unlimited subscribers on the free plan

Choose Buttondown if...

  • You prefer writing in markdown for efficiency and technical consistency
  • You need automation workflows and the ability to manage multiple newsletters
  • You want predictable pricing without revenue-share and better customer reviews

Frequently asked questions

Is Substack better than Buttondown?

Neither is objectively 'better'—it depends on your needs. Substack excels for creators focused on monetization and audience building, offering unlimited free subscribers and a built-in community. Buttondown is better for technical writers and developers who value markdown support, API access, and transparent pricing. Buttondown has significantly higher customer satisfaction ratings (4.8★ vs 4.0★ on G2).

Which is cheaper, Substack or Buttondown?

Substack is free indefinitely, even with unlimited subscribers—you only pay when readers subscribe to paid tiers (10% commission). Buttondown charges $9-$99/month based on subscriber count, making it more expensive for larger lists. For monetization, Substack's revenue-share model means lower upfront costs, while Buttondown's fixed pricing offers predictability without taking a cut of your revenue.

Can I switch from Buttondown to Substack?

Yes, both platforms support import/export functionality, making migration possible. Buttondown explicitly lists import/export as a key feature, and most email platforms allow you to export subscriber lists. However, you'll need to manually recreate designs, automations, and custom settings on the new platform. Plan your migration carefully to maintain subscriber relationships and avoid duplicate sends.
